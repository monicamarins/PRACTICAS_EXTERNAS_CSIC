About the Project

This research project, developed by the CSIC, focuses on the monitoring and analysis of volcanic activity in the Canary Islands. The project particularly investigates the Tajogaite volcano eruption in La Palma in 2021, as well as continuous monitoring of the Teide volcano in Tenerife.

We are Physics students from the University of La Laguna, currently undertaking our company internship program (Pr√°cticas de Empresa). This project is part of our training and academic development.

Our team, in collaboration with the CSIC and the University of La Laguna, combines various techniques such as remote sensing, data analysis, and scientific visualization to better understand volcanic processes and improve early warning systems.

Project Goals

Thermal Analysis

Analyze brightness temperature and radiance patterns, monitoring the fumarole fields of Teide volcano, the Tajogaite Volcano in La Palma and Timanfaya in Lanzarote using thermal infrared data.

Earthquake Correlation

Analyze earthquake-eruption correlations with a custom trigger index based on distance between the epicenter of the events, the volcano we are studying and the magnitude of these events.

Data Screening

Perform seismic filtering to identify signs of volcanic activation with ~5 year prediction window.

Technical Development

Create Python tools for satellite data processing, automated visualization pipelines and ML models for eruption prediction.

Understanding the Thermal Activity Graphs

This visualization shows the evolution of thermal energy emitted by the Tajogaite volcano following its eruption (September 19 ‚Äì December 13, 2021). The metric used is Radiative Power (FRP), which estimates the amount of thermal energy radiated into the atmosphere and is directly linked to surface volcanic activity (lava flows, hot gases, fumaroles, etc.).

Immediately after the eruption, the volcanic terrain retains extremely high temperatures (e.g. incandescent lava, fresh flows), which results in high FRP values. However, in the following days, the volcanic system begins to cool rapidly due to:

  • The absence of new magma being emitted
  • Natural dissipation of heat into the atmosphere
  • Solidification and consolidation of volcanic material

Even though the volcano enters a dormant phase, fumaroles, persistent hot spots, and residual geochemical processes may continue to generate weaker but still detectable thermal signals for months or even years after the eruption.

Geological Background

The island of La Palma, experiences seismic activity related to regional tectonics. Between October 2017 and June 2021, eight similar seismic swarms were recorded in the southern part of the island.

A 2021 scientific study using advanced satellite techniques detected anomalies in the subsurface of the Aridane Valley since 2009-2010, suggesting possible volcanic reactivation. However, the study itself noted that in 80% of cases with these types of signals, no eruptions occur.

On September 11, 2021, a new low-intensity seismic swarm began. By Monday the 13th, about 1,500 seismic movements had been recorded, concentrated in the Cumbre Vieja Natural Park.

On September 19, the day the eruption began, more than 300 earthquakes were detected, including one of magnitude 3.8 mbLg at shallow depth. Additionally, ground deformation of up to 15 cm was recorded due to magma pressure.

Volcanic-Seismic Relation

Seismic-volcanic correlation

There are several ways earthquakes affect volcanic activity. The main cases are those related to the dynamic and static stresses that they cause to the magmatic chambers. Although the event may also tear the chamber if the fault is large enough or if the hypocenter is close enough.

These are not the only conditions to cause an eruptive period, there has to be a "predisposition" to ignite it. The main things are the material composition of the crust, the viscosity and density of the magma, the bubble flow, if there are temperature changes that affects to the (usually crystal) ceiling of the magmatic chamber...

Statistically, closed system volcanoes are more likely to trigger due to seismic events than open volcanoes, existing a geological factor that affects to this statistic. Specifically, it is estimated that in Chile and Indonesia 25% of volcanoes are susceptible to earthquakes (Watt et al. 2009) while in other regions is estimated to be only 0.3-0.4% (Linde and Selwyn Sacks, 1998; Manga and Brodsky, 2006; Lemarchand and Grasso, 2007).

Usually the time-span accepted to consider an earthquake the cause of an eruption is up to 5 years, due to the studied time-span of stabilization of the volcano after the different stress-caused mechanisms. It has been seen that in this range the number of volcanoes activated by earthquakes is larger than looking up to 2 years and smaller periods.

Volcanic activity

When starting periods of volcanic activity, it is usual to register a larger number of seismic events due to the vibrations caused by the flow of magma, gases, and other fluids present in the magmatic chambers.

These events are not considered the cause of the volcanic eruption but a consequence of its activity. These are characterized by their proximity to the volcano.

It is usually seen that non-volcanic earthquakes are those with an epicenter further than 25 km from it.

Teide's Thermal Signals

The thermal activity at Teide volcano primarily originates from its extensive fumarole fields, where gases escape from the volcanic system at temperatures ranging from ambient to over 86¬∞C.

Between July 2024 and January 2025, satellite data revealed elevated thermal emissions from these fumaroles, corresponding with increased seismic and geodetic activity in the region.

Key factors influencing the thermal signals:

  • Seasonal temperature variations affecting surface measurements
  • Changes in hydrothermal system pressure
  • Weather conditions impacting satellite observations

Understanding Teide's Thermal Signals

The graph above illustrates the Daily Radiative Power (MW) at Teide from 2021 to 2025. It clearly shows a steady increase in fumarolic thermal output starting in early 2022, peaking in late 2024. The highest value recorded was 146 MW around November 2024, which aligns with the major seismic swarm detected on November 14, 2024‚Äîan event involving over 500 microearthquakes. This temporal coincidence suggests a possible link between subsurface pressure changes and the intensified thermal anomalies captured in the satellite observations.

Notably, on November 14, 2024, the National Geographic Institute (IGN) detected an anomalous seismic swarm comprising over 500 microearthquakes southwest of Pico Viejo within a three-hour window. These events, occurring at depths of 8‚Äì12 km, suggest increased pressure within Teide's hydrothermal system (Canarian Weekly, 14 Nov 2024), (DA News, 14 Nov 2024).

Additionally, since 2023, GPS measurements have indicated a ground uplift of approximately one centimetre, and a slight increase in CO‚ÇÇ emissions has been observed in various areas of Tenerife, including Teide's crater (Diario de Avisos, 24 Jan 2025), (Canarian Weekly, 26 Jan 2025).

While Teide remains in a dormant phase, continuous monitoring of these thermal signals aids scientists in detecting any changes that might indicate increasing volcanic activity.
